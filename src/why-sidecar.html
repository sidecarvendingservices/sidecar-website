How Restocking Actually Works

We catch it before it's empty.

Our team monitors every machine's inventory in real time — not just when something's completely sold out, but when a single item is trending low. That's the target: eliminate out-of-stocks entirely, and treat a low-stock item as a priority for restocking and, when it's warranted, an extra delivery run rather than waiting for the next regularly scheduled visit. A machine running on fumes for two days before someone notices is exactly the failure mode this is built to prevent.

Who Shows Up

Owner-operators, not a route driver.

Right now, restocking is done by Vala and Brian personally — not a rotating route driver working through a list of accounts. That's deliberate. Every restock is also a chance to check in with property managers and employees, make sure everything's actually running smoothly, and hear directly what's working and what people wish was stocked instead. Product mixes get refreshed based on that feedback, not a generic planogram — because the people restocking your machine are the same people who'd have to answer for it if it wasn't right.

We also build a formal 30-day check-in into every installation — a sit-down with your property manager or site contact about a month in, to look at what's actually selling and adjust anything that's not working before it becomes a habit.

Retail Instincts, Applied To Vending

We know what it takes to earn repeat business.

Years in retail taught us that a fresh, relevant product mix and a clean, fully stocked presentation aren't details — they're the difference between a machine people use and one they walk past. None of that is complicated, but it's exactly the kind of thing a route-based vending operator tends to overlook — and it's the difference between a customer using a machine once and using it every day.
